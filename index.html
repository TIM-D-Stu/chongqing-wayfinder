The 8D Problem

Chongqing has more than 32 million people on steep mountains where the Yangtze and Jialing rivers meet. There was never enough flat land to spread out, so the city grew upward. Roads stack on top of roads. The Liziba monorail passes through a residential building. One building's first floor can be another side's twenty-second.

You can stand on the right spot on a map and still be on the wrong level. Ordinary GPS apps don't know the difference. This app does.

How Routing Works

The city is abstracted into a weighted graph. Each landmark or transfer point is a node with an elevation. Each walkable connection — a path, elevator, skybridge, stairs, monorail line — is an edge.

To find a route, the app runs Dijkstra's algorithm, but the cost of an edge isn't only horizontal distance. It also includes vertical effort, with stairs costing the most per floor and elevators almost nothing. That's why the route you get is the easiest one for a pedestrian, not just the shortest line on the map.

Computational Thinking

Abstraction The real city becomes a graph of nodes with elevations and edges with types.
Algorithms Dijkstra's shortest-path search with an effort-weighted cost function.
Data Landmarks and edges live in a structured dataset, separate from the program logic.
Interaction Event-driven UI. Pick two landmarks, click a button, see the route.

Project

AP Computer Science Principles · Final Innovation Project. The physical artefact is a tri-fold card whose QR code launches this app. Together they bridge Chongqing's vertical city with a tool that finally understands height, layers, and landmarks — not just latitude and longitude.
